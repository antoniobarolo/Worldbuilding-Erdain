Star System

The planet of Erdain is located in a Type-S Binary Star System, in which two stars, Narháera and Ghaláera, orbit around a center of mass at a distance of around 80 to 100 Astronomical Units (in earthly measurements).

These stars weren't arranged together since they were born though. Our planet orbits around Narháera, which may be only half as massive as its partner, but is more than three times as old. As it drifted through the galaxy, its orbit met young Ghaláera's by around 1.28 billion Erdain-Years (or 800 million Earth-Years) before the development of Hominid Societies, creating a binary system which seems to be gradually pulling the stars closer together.

Narháera

Narháera is the star around the planet of Erdain orbits. Its color frequencies peak at a light green. Our planet receives similar amounts of heat from its star as the Earth receives from the Sun, despite Narháera having a lower mass and temperature. This is because our planet orbits considerably closer to its star. The implication of this though is that the planet of Erdain is subject to more radiation, which is at least slightly, but not fully, compensated by its thicker atmosphere.

Ghaláera

Ghaláera is the largest of the two stars, but since it is around 100 times further from our planet than Narháera, it looks much smaller in the sky. Its color frequencies peak from lilac to ultraviolet. Because of this long distance, Ghaláera's effects on our planet's climate are much more subtle, but still very noticeable, such as creating two more seasons in certain latitudes of the globe in which leaves grow in a different color. Its rays of radiation seem to have influenced evolution of life on our planet in many ways, sometimes by provoquing exctintion, others by accelerating genetic variability.

Because of our planet orbiting only Narháera, the angle of Ghaláera in the sky changes completely during the year. The dawn and dusk of both stars in the sky get in and out of sync as our planet proceeds its translation, even at the equator where the planet's acute axial tilt is not a factor. In higher latitudes, these stars can dance on the firmament quite wildly.
